# Skill: Offer Audit (Weak Point Detector)

## When to Use
- An offer is not converting, conversions are low, or something feels off
- You want to improve an existing offer before launch or after disappointing results
- You need a structured diagnosis of why an offer underperforms, with prioritized fixes

## Core Framework

The audit is grounded in the Value Equation from $100M Offers Ch.6:

```
Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice)
```

Increase the top two, decrease the bottom two. If the denominator reaches zero, value becomes infinite. "Beginner marketers focus on bigger claims (top of equation). Advanced marketers focus on reducing time delay and effort (bottom of equation) -- like Apple, Amazon, Netflix." ($100M Offers Ch.6)

The audit evaluates the offer across 15 dimensions organized into five layers:

1. **Value Equation** (4 dimensions): Dream Outcome, Perceived Likelihood, Time Delay, Effort & Sacrifice
2. **Pricing Strategy** (1 dimension): Virtuous Cycle of Price position
3. **Enhancement Stack** (5 dimensions): Scarcity, Urgency, Bonuses, Guarantees, Naming
4. **Market & Structure** (3 dimensions): Niche Depth, Commoditization Check, Offer Construction (Splinter Stack)
5. **Completeness** (2 dimensions): Problem-Solution Exhaustion, Sales-to-Fulfillment Continuum Position

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Extract the Offer

Identify and summarize:
- Who it is for (the avatar)
- What it promises (the dream outcome)
- How it works (the delivery vehicle)
- Price
- Format (DFY / DWY / DIY)

Summarize in one sentence:

> This offer helps [avatar] achieve [dream outcome] using [delivery vehicle] for [price].

---

### Step 2: Commoditization Check ($100M Offers Ch.2-3)

This is the foundational test. "When prospects can compare your product to alternatives and think 'these are the same, I will buy the cheaper one,' you are commoditized." ($100M Offers Ch.3)

Evaluate:
- Can this offer be directly compared to a competitor's offer?
- Is the prospect choosing between this and something else, or between this and nothing?
- Does it sell on VALUE or PRICE?

A Grand Slam Offer "cannot be compared to any other product or service" and lets you sell in a "category of one." ($100M Offers Ch.2)

"The same business doing the same fulfillment work can appear to have a totally different product simply by using a Grand Slam Offer instead of a commodity offer." ($100M Offers Ch.3)

**Scoring:**
- 1-3 (critical): Offer is directly comparable to competitors; prospect shops on price
- 4-6 (needs work): Some differentiation but core components feel interchangeable
- 7-8 (solid): Meaningful differentiation; hard to compare apples-to-apples
- 9-10 (strong): Category of one; purchasing decision is between this offer and nothing

If the score is 5 or below, flag this as the top priority fix -- everything else is secondary until the offer escapes commodity territory.

---

### Step 3: Niche Depth Evaluation ($100M Offers Ch.4)

"A starving crowd beats everything." Evaluate the four Starving Crowd indicators:
- **Massive Pain**: Do they desperately need this? "The degree of pain is proportional to the price you can charge."
- **Purchasing Power**: Can they afford what you charge?
- **Easy to Target**: Can you find and reach them through specific channels?
- **Growing**: Is this market growing or declining? "There is no such thing as maintaining." (Grow Or Die principle)

Then evaluate niche specificity using Niche Pricing Escalation ($100M Offers Ch.4):
- How specific is the avatar? "The same product can be priced 100x higher when targeted to a specific avatar."
- Example from the book: "Time Management" at $19 vs "Time Management for B2B Outbound Power Tools Sales Reps" at $2,000. The core content is nearly identical but the specificity commands 100x the price.

**Scoring:**
- 1-3: Avatar is "everyone" or a broad demographic; no niche specificity
- 4-6: Some specificity but could be niched further to command premium pricing
- 7-8: Clear, specific avatar with strong pain, money, and accessibility
- 9-10: Hyper-specific niche where the prospect thinks "this was made exactly for me"

Check for the **Niche Slap** warning: "Entrepreneurs hop from niche to niche thinking the market is the problem, when the real problem is they have not committed long enough to find their Grand Slam Offer." ($100M Offers Ch.4) If the user is considering switching niches, challenge whether they should fix the offer instead.

### Step 4: Pricing Strategy -- Virtuous Cycle of Price ($100M Offers Ch.5, Gym Launch Secrets)

This evaluates whether the offer's pricing triggers the virtuous cycle or the vicious cycle.

**The Virtuous Cycle**: "Raising prices increases client emotional investment, perceived value, actual results (because they pay more attention), attracts best clients who are easiest to satisfy, and multiplies margin for reinvestment." ($100M Offers Ch.5)

**The Vicious Cycle**: "Lowering prices decreases emotional investment, perceived value, results, attracts worst clients, and destroys margin needed to deliver excellence." ($100M Offers Ch.5)

Key principles to evaluate against:
- "There is no strategic benefit to being the second cheapest in the marketplace, but there is for being the most expensive." (Dan Kennedy, cited in $100M Offers Ch.5)
- "Those who pay the most, pay the most attention. If clients must do something to achieve results, getting them maximally invested through pricing ensures adherence." ($100M Offers Ch.5)
- "Higher price literally means higher perceived value" -- the blind wine study shows the same wine is rated better when labeled expensive. ($100M Offers Ch.5)
- "People want to buy expensive things -- they just need a reason." ($100M Offers Ch.5)
- "The goal is not to be slightly above market -- it is to be so much higher that the consumer thinks 'there must be something entirely different going on here.' That creates a category of one with monopoly profits." ($100M Offers Ch.5)

From Gym Launch Secrets: "The cheapest customers are always the most demanding; the highest-paying are the most easygoing." And: "People will not give you their time until they give you their money."

Evaluate:
- Is the offer priced to trigger the virtuous cycle or the vicious cycle?
- Is it priced to be "the most expensive" in its category, or is it competing on price?
- Does the price create enough margin to reinvest in better delivery?
- Can the seller state the price with conviction? "Charge as high a price as you can say out loud without cracking a smile." (Dan Kennedy, cited in $100M Offers Ch.5)

**Scoring:**
- 1-3: Priced low to "compete"; vicious cycle dynamics present (worst clients, thin margins, declining quality)
- 4-6: Market-rate pricing; neither triggering virtuous nor vicious cycle strongly
- 7-8: Premium pricing with conviction; margin allows reinvestment; better clients attracted
- 9-10: Category-defining price that signals "something entirely different is going on here"; full virtuous cycle in motion

### Step 5: Value Equation Analysis ($100M Offers Ch.6)

Score each of the four drivers 1-10:

#### 5a. Dream Outcome
- Is the result clear, specific, and desirable?
- Is it framed in terms of status? "Frame benefits in terms of how others will perceive the prospect's achievement." ($100M Offers Ch.6)
- Does it sell the destination, not the process? "No one wants your membership, your software, your consulting hours. They want the outcome -- the weight loss, the revenue growth, the peace of mind." ($100M Offers, mental model). In the CLOSER formula from Gym Launch Secrets, this principle is illustrated vividly: "Sell them the vacation, not the flight. No one wants to hear about TSA lines, baggage claim, delays, layovers, or time changes. They want to hear about Maui." (Gym Launch Secrets Ch.6 -- CLOSER formula, "S" step)

Weak signs: vague outcomes ("grow", "improve"), no measurable result, feature-focused instead of outcome-focused.

#### 5b. Perceived Likelihood of Achievement
- Is there proof (testimonials, case studies, metrics)?
- Is the path clear and believable?
- Does experience back the claims? "A surgeon's 10,000th patient pays far more than the first." ($100M Offers Ch.6)

Weak signs: no testimonials, unclear process, big promises without supporting evidence.

#### 5c. Time Delay
- How fast do results happen?
- When does the first win occur?
- "Fast beats free. The only thing that beats 'free' is 'fast.'" ($100M Offers Ch.6)
- Are there short-term experience wins? "Always try and incorporate short-term, immediate wins for a client. They just need to know they are on the right path." ($100M Offers Ch.6)

Weak signs: long delay before any results, no quick wins designed into the experience, no onboarding momentum.

#### 5d. Effort & Sacrifice
- How hard does it feel for the prospect?
- How many steps, how much discipline required?
- "In an ideal world, the prospect says yes and the outcome happens with zero additional effort." ($100M Offers Ch.6)
- People pay more for effortless solutions: "People pay $200 for supplements but will not pay $29/month for a gym membership." ($100M Offers Ch.6)

Weak signs: too many steps, unclear instructions, heavy effort, no DFY or automation options.

### Step 6: Unsolved Problem Scan ($100M Offers Ch.9)

"You must resolve every obstacle a buyer believes they will have. A single unsolved 'one thing' can prevent a sale." ($100M Offers Ch.9)

Walk through the Problem-Solution Exhaustion Method:
1. What is the dream outcome?
2. List every single thing the prospect must do to achieve it
3. For each thing, list every reason they would fail (mapping to the four value drivers)
4. Check: does the offer have a solution for EVERY problem identified?

You should find 32-64 sub-problems. Any single missing solution can kill a sale.

"When Hormozi was forced by financial necessity to solve a prospect's 'eating out' objection, he created an asset that helped close countless future sales." ($100M Offers Ch.9)

**Scoring:**
- 1-3: Multiple obvious unsolved problems; prospect can easily think of reasons it will not work for them
- 4-6: Most common problems addressed but gaps remain
- 7-8: Comprehensive coverage; only edge cases unaddressed
- 9-10: Every conceivable obstacle has a named solution

---

### Step 7: Offer Construction -- Splinter Stack Check (Gym Launch Secrets Ch.2, $100M Offers Ch.10)

This evaluates whether the offer has been constructed using the Irresistible Offer Checklist from Gym Launch Secrets -- an 8-step framework for making an offer so compelling that prospects cannot compare it to competitors:

1. **Pre-Frame**: Has authority and social proof been positioned before the prospect arrives? "The sale starts before they pick up the phone." (Gym Launch Secrets Ch.2)
2. **Price Anchor**: Is a higher price point dropped before presenting the actual price so it seems like a deal by comparison?
3. **Splinter Stack**: Has the offer been unbundled into individually named components with assigned dollar values? "Unbundle everything you already do and list each item separately to make the offer feel massive." (Gym Launch Secrets Ch.2). This is distinct from bonuses -- the splinter stack is the core offer broken into visible pieces.
4. **Scarcity**: Are there limited spots or real capacity constraints communicated?
5. **Urgency**: Is there time-based pressure with a real deadline?
6. **Crazy Guarantee**: Is risk fully reversed? "Best case you change their life; worst case they worked out for free." (Gym Launch Secrets Ch.2)
7. **Bribe**: Is there an incentive for immediate action (e.g., the program is free if they sign up for membership today)?
8. **Downsell Your Upsell**: Is the initial high-ticket price followed by an ongoing option at a lower rate, making continuity feel like a bargain? "Charge double at the start when excitement is highest, then make ongoing membership seem like a bargain at half the price." (Gym Launch Secrets Ch.2)

The key question: has the offer been "decommoditized" by combining services in a unique way that makes it immune to price comparison?

**Scoring:**
- 1-3: Offer is presented as a single lump sum with no visible components; easy to comparison-shop
- 4-6: Some components are named but no price anchoring, no splinter stack, or missing several checklist items
- 7-8: Strong splinter stack with named components and dollar values; most checklist items present
- 9-10: All 8 checklist items present; offer feels massive, unique, and incomparable

---

### Step 8: Enhancement Stack Audit ($100M Offers Ch.11-16)

These are five psychological levers applied ON TOP of the core offer. Evaluate each separately:

#### 8a. Scarcity ($100M Offers Ch.12)

Three types to check for:
- **Limited supply of seats/slots**: Total Business Cap, Growth Rate Cap, or Cohort Cap
- **Limited supply of bonuses**: Specific bonuses only available to the first N buyers
- **Never available again**: This exact offer will not be repeated

"Fear of loss is stronger than desire for gain." ($100M Offers Ch.12)

Is scarcity honest? "Simply defining your actual capacity and advertising it" is the most ethical and effective form. ($100M Offers Ch.12)

**Scoring:**
- 1-3: No scarcity mechanism at all; unlimited availability signals low demand
- 4-6: Some scarcity but feels manufactured or is not communicated
- 7-8: Real, honest scarcity clearly communicated
- 9-10: Scarcity compounds over time; you consistently sell out and announce it

#### 8b. Urgency ($100M Offers Ch.13)

Four types to check for:
- **Cohort-Based Rolling**: "Next group starts Monday"
- **Rolling Seasonal**: Same promotion renamed by season with real deadlines
- **Pricing/Bonus-Based**: A promotion or discount they could miss
- **Exploding Opportunity**: Arbitrage that decays with time

"The last 3% of time allotted in a campaign creates 50-60% of sales." ($100M Offers Ch.13)

**Scoring:**
- 1-3: No deadline, no time pressure; prospect can "think about it" forever
- 4-6: Some urgency but deadlines feel arbitrary or fake
- 7-8: Real deadlines with clear consequences for missing them
- 9-10: Multiple urgency layers reinforcing each other

#### 8c. Bonuses ($100M Offers Ch.14)

Check for:
- Are bonuses present at all?
- Does the total bonus value eclipse the core offer value?
- Are bonuses named with benefit-driven names and assigned dollar values?
- Are tools and checklists used (they beat additional trainings as bonus formats)?
- Is there Partner Bonus Arbitrage? "Get adjacent businesses to give you their products or services as bonuses in exchange for exposure to your customer base." ($100M Offers Ch.14)

"Never discount the main offer. It teaches your customers that your prices are negotiable. Adding bonuses to increase value to close the deal is far superior to cutting prices." ($100M Offers Ch.14)

**Scoring:**
- 1-3: No bonuses, or bonuses are just repackaged parts of the core offer
- 4-6: Some bonuses but weak names, no assigned values, or low perceived value
- 7-8: Named bonuses with dollar values that meaningfully increase total perceived value
- 9-10: Bonus value eclipses core offer value; includes partner bonuses at zero cost

#### 8d. Guarantees ($100M Offers Ch.15)

Four types to check for:
- **Unconditional**: No questions asked refund
- **Conditional**: Terms tied to key success actions
- **Anti-Guarantee**: All sales final with compelling damaging admission ("Our methods are so powerful that once you see them, they cannot be unseen")
- **Implied**: Performance-based or revenue-share models

Best option: **Service Guarantee** -- keep working free of charge until the result is achieved. "Since I have been advising businesses to use this particular guarantee, I have yet to have a single person say a client took them up on it." ($100M Offers Ch.15)

The guarantee math almost always works in your favor: "A strong guarantee that increases sales by 30% while doubling refund rate from 5% to 10% still nets 23% more revenue." ($100M Offers Ch.15) Calculate: 130 sales with 13 refunds (10%) = 117 net vs 100 sales with 5 refunds (5%) = 95 net. That is a 23% increase in pure profit.

Can guarantees be stacked? Multiple guarantees compound perceived safety.

Is the guarantee named? "Give your guarantee a memorable name." ($100M Offers Ch.15)

**Scoring:**
- 1-3: No guarantee at all; all risk sits with the buyer
- 4-6: Basic money-back guarantee but not named, not prominent, or not compelling
- 7-8: Strong guarantee (conditional or service-based) clearly communicated and named
- 9-10: Stacked guarantees that make the prospect feel risk is genuinely zero

#### 8e. Naming -- M-A-G-I-C Formula ($100M Offers Ch.16)

Check the offer name against the five components:
- **M** -- Make a Magnetic Reason Why: Free, 88% off, Grand Opening, New Year, Anniversary
- **A** -- Announce Your Avatar: Call out exactly who you are looking for
- **G** -- Give Them A Goal: Articulate the dream outcome
- **I** -- Indicate a Time Interval: Duration that sets expectations and creates urgency
- **C** -- Complete With A Container Word: Challenge, Blueprint, Bootcamp, Intensive, Masterclass, System, Accelerator, Sprint

"Use 3-5 components. The shorter and punchier the better. Rhyme and alliterate when possible." ($100M Offers Ch.16)

Also check: are individual components (bonuses, guarantees, features) named with benefit-driven names? "Instead of 'Meal Plan,' name it 'Personalized Lick-Your-Fingers-Good Meal Plan.'" ($100M Offers Ch.14)

"The same core offer with a new name can get 2x, 3x, or 10x the response rate." ($100M Offers Ch.16)

**Scoring:**
- 1-3: Generic, descriptive name ("Module 3: Nutrition", "Our Consulting Package")
- 4-6: Some benefit language but missing avatar, interval, or container word
- 7-8: Strong name using 3-4 M-A-G-I-C components; bonuses also named
- 9-10: Punchy, memorable name using 4-5 components; every element of the offer is named

---

### Step 9: Sales-to-Fulfillment Continuum Position ($100M Offers Ch.10)

"Create flow, monetize flow, then add friction." ($100M Offers Ch.10)

Evaluate where the offer sits on the continuum:
- **Early stage** (over-delivering, learning): Is the business generating cash flow and building case studies? Are they doing whatever it takes to get results? This is correct for early-stage.
- **Growth stage** (systematizing): Are they turning proven one-on-one processes into one-to-many delivery vehicles? "High value one-to-many solutions have high one-time creation cost but infinitely low ongoing cost." ($100M Offers Ch.10)
- **Optimization stage** (adding friction): Are they only adding friction (reducing fulfillment cost) after demand is established?

Check for the common mistake: trying to build the perfectly efficient, scalable system BEFORE having clients. "Efficiency without demand is pointless. Get clients first, optimize second." ($100M Offers Ch.10)

Also apply the 10x / 1-10th Stretch Test ($100M Offers Ch.10):
- If clients paid 10x the price, what would you provide?
- If they paid 1/10th and you had to make it MORE valuable, how?
- The answers reveal creative delivery vehicles the business has not considered.

**Scoring:**
- 1-3: Mismatched -- either over-optimizing before having demand, or still doing unsustainable over-delivery at scale
- 4-6: Roughly appropriate for stage but not deliberately managed
- 7-8: Consciously positioned with a clear plan for progression
- 9-10: Optimized for current stage with documented path to next stage

---

### Step 10: Messaging & Belief Check

#### 10a. Messaging Quick Check
- Does the messaging make the prospect feel understood? "The point of good persuasion is for the prospect to feel understood, not for them to understand you." ($100M Offers Ch.6)
- Is it framed around status? "Frame benefits in terms of how others will perceive the prospect's achievement." ($100M Offers Ch.6)
- Does it sell the destination, not the process? "Sell them the vacation, not the flight. No one wants to hear about TSA lines, baggage claim, delays, layovers, or time changes. They want to hear about Maui." (Gym Launch Secrets Ch.6 -- CLOSER formula)

#### 10b. Belief-Breaking Check (Gym Launch Secrets Ch.6)

Does the offer's messaging break false beliefs prospects hold about why they failed before? The Belief-Breaking Formula is a five-step persuasion framework:

1. Say what they believe (e.g., "So you believe cheap front-end offers work?")
2. Say why it is wrong (e.g., "This does not provide cash flow to market or acquire customers")
3. Say what is right (e.g., "Use a high-ticket front-end acquisition")
4. Say WHY it is right (e.g., "It will allow you to spend whatever you need to get people in profitably")
5. Show proof -- third-party stats, testimonials, wall of results

If the offer's sales copy or pitch does not address why previous solutions failed for the prospect, it is missing a powerful conversion lever. Prospects who have tried and failed carry beliefs about why they failed -- if you do not break those beliefs, they will apply the same skepticism to your offer.

---

### Step 11: Synthesize and Prioritize

After scoring all 15 dimensions, identify:
1. The single weakest dimension (this is the bottleneck)
2. The top 3 fixes by impact
3. Quick wins (can be implemented in 24-48 hours)

## Common Mistakes

- **Skipping the Commoditization Check**: If the offer can be comparison-shopped, no amount of Enhancement Stack work will fix it. Differentiation comes first. ($100M Offers Ch.2-3)
- **Trying to fix everything at once**: Fix the single weakest dimension first. One strong improvement beats five mediocre ones.
- **Discounting instead of adding bonuses**: "Discounting your main offer teaches customers that your prices are negotiable and puts you in a position of weakness." ($100M Offers Ch.14)
- **Avoiding guarantees out of fear of refunds**: Run the math. 130 sales with 10% refund rate nets 117 sales; 100 sales with 5% refund rate nets 95 sales. That is 23% more revenue with the stronger guarantee. ($100M Offers Ch.15)
- **Generic naming**: "A named component with a dollar value assigned is perceived as dramatically more valuable than the same thing unnamed." ($100M Offers Ch.14)
- **Switching niches instead of fixing the offer**: "The instinct when something is not working is to change markets. The correct move is usually to change the offer within the same market." (Niche Slap Principle, $100M Offers Ch.4)
- **Building perfect systems before having demand**: "Create flow, monetize flow, then add friction." ($100M Offers Ch.10)
- **Leaving any problem unsolved**: "A single unsolved 'one thing' can prevent a sale. Don't be romantic about how you solve problems." ($100M Offers Ch.9)
- **Pricing in the vicious cycle**: Low prices attract the worst clients, produce the worst results, and destroy margin needed for excellence. "The cheapest customers are always the most demanding; the highest-paying are the most easygoing." (Gym Launch Secrets)
- **Presenting the offer as a single lump**: Unbundle everything into a splinter stack with named components and assigned dollar values. The same offer presented as 8 named components feels massively more valuable than one line item. (Gym Launch Secrets Ch.2)
- **Ignoring false beliefs**: If the prospect has tried and failed before, they carry beliefs about why they failed. If your messaging does not break those beliefs, they will apply the same skepticism to your offer. (Belief-Breaking Formula, Gym Launch Secrets Ch.6)
