# Skill: Referral Engine

## When to Use

- The user's business depends on word of mouth but has no system for generating referrals deliberately
- Churn exceeds referrals, meaning the business shrinks without constant new advertising
- The user wants to build a compounding customer base where referrals > churn eliminates the need for other advertising

## Context: Referrals Within the Four Lead Getters ($100M Leads)

Customer Referrals are one of four lead getter types that create leverage beyond what any single person can do:

1. **Customer Referrals** -- lowest cost, exponential growth potential. Referrals > Churn = compounding business
2. **Employees** -- train using Document-Demonstrate-Duplicate (3Ds) to run your business without you
3. **Agencies** -- use them to LEARN new methods and platforms, not as permanent outsourced advertising
4. **Affiliates** -- independent businesses that tell their audience to buy your stuff

Lead getters start as leads themselves -- you use the Core Four (warm outreach, free content, cold outreach, paid ads) to get them. The process repeats: lead getters can get lead getters (employees who hire, affiliates who recruit affiliates).

This skill focuses on Customer Referrals -- the highest-quality, lowest-cost lead source. If referrals exceed churn, the business compounds without other advertising.

## Core Framework

### The Goodwill Equation ($100M Leads Ch.12)

```
Goodwill = Value Delivered - Price Charged
```

> "Price is what you charge. Value is what they get. The difference between price and value is goodwill."
> -- Alex Hormozi, $100M Leads

Goodwill drives word of mouth. The larger the gap between value delivered and price charged, the more referrals you generate. The goal is to maximize this gap -- not by lowering price, but by increasing value delivered.

> "Any fool can sell something for less."
> -- Rory Sutherland, quoted in $100M Leads

### The Compounding Business Test

```
If Referrals > Churn --> business compounds without any other advertising
If Referrals < Churn --> business shrinks without constant new advertising
```

This is the single most important metric for long-term business health. A business where referrals exceed churn grows on autopilot.

### The Pie Equation (Gym Launch Secrets Ch.13-16)

```
Hypothetical Max Customers (HGM) = New Sign-ups Per Month / Monthly Churn Rate
```

Example: 10 sign-ups / 10% churn = 100 max customers. 20 sign-ups / 5% churn = 400 max customers (4x improvement from changing both variables).

Key insight: Reducing churn from 10% to 5% doubles your maximum customer base -- the same effect as doubling sign-ups. Reducing churn from 9% to 3% is not a 6% improvement -- it is a 300% increase in lifetime value. Manipulating both sign-ups AND churn simultaneously creates multiplicative growth, not just additive.

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Run the One-Customer Thought Experiment

Before building any referral system, run this diagnostic from $100M Leads:

> "Imagine you lost all customers but one. The gods of advertising ban you from doing the Core Four. All future customers must come from this one customer. How would you treat them? What results would they need? What would onboarding look like? Write it out. Then do it for every customer."

Ask the user:
1. If your entire business depended on one customer referring all future customers, what would you change about your delivery?
2. What results would that customer need to achieve?
3. What would onboarding look like?
4. How often would you communicate with them?
5. What would the experience feel like?

> "Start acting like the advertising gods will revoke your core four privileges at any moment."
> -- Alex Hormozi, $100M Leads

Write out the answers. This becomes the standard for every customer.

### Step 2: Diagnose Current Goodwill Using the Six Levers

Each of the six ways to increase goodwill maps to a component of the advertising system. Walk the user through each lever:

**Lever 1: Sell Better Customers (maps to Call Outs)**
- Who gets the most value from your product?
- Are you targeting more of them specifically?
- Better customers get better results, which generates more goodwill

**Lever 2: Set Better Expectations (maps to Dream Outcome)**
- Are you over-promising to close sales?
- Lower promises create room to overdeliver
- The fastest, easiest, and cheapest way to make your product remarkable is to make it better than they expect

> "Slowly inch down your promises until close rates start to dip. This maximizes both customer acquisition and the goodwill that drives referrals."
> -- $100M Leads

**Lever 3: Get More People Better Results (maps to Perceived Likelihood)**
- What do your best customers do differently?
- Can you force everyone to do what your best customers do?
- Find the behaviors that produce results, then systematize them

**Lever 4: Make Faster Wins (maps to Time Delay)**
- Are you delivering small wins frequently, or one big win at the end?
- Force first wins within 48 hours of purchase
- Customers form their lasting impression within the first 48 hours after they buy
- Set many expectations. Meet many expectations. Repeat
- Add 50% to timelines so you always deliver early
- Never expect customers to forgive you

**Lever 5: Keep Making Stuff Better (maps to Effort/Sacrifice)**
- Do you have a monthly cycle of: survey customers, identify top complaints, fix them, implement, measure?
- Continuous improvement reduces effort required from the customer

**Lever 6: Tell Them What to Buy Next (maps to CTA)**
- Do customers always know their next step with you?
- Keep selling so they do not fall off
- A customer who has nothing left to buy drifts away (editorial paraphrase -- the book's language is "keep selling so they do not fall off")

### Step 3: Implement BAMFAM ($100M Money Models)

BAMFAM = Book A Meeting From A Meeting

> "Never leave a customer in no man's land. They should always know what happens next."
> -- Alex Hormozi

> "A customer should know the next time they see you -- and why -- before they leave."
> -- Sharran Srivatsaa, quoted in $100M Money Models

Rule: Never end any customer interaction without scheduling the next one. This creates a continuous chain that prevents customers from drifting away and creates natural upsell opportunities.

At the end of every client call, meeting, or deliverable: "Great, let us schedule our next check-in. How does [date] at [time] work?"

BAMFAM serves three purposes:
1. Retention -- customers never enter no-man's-land
2. Upsell opportunity -- each meeting is a chance to solve the next problem
3. Referral timing -- engaged customers refer more than disengaged ones

### Step 4: Deploy the Five Horsemen of Retention (Gym Launch Secrets Ch.13-16)

Five specific tactics that reduce monthly churn from 10%+ to under 3% within two to three months. These are not theories -- they are the common practices found across all top-performing businesses in Hormozi's portfolio.

**Horseman 1: Reach-Outs**
- Personally text or message every customer every 14 days as a pulse check
- Systematize but never automate -- genuine communication attracts attention
- Use spreadsheets, CRM lists, and assigned customer groups per team member

> "Communication is the best content. My recommendation is to SYSTEMATIZE communication, but never AUTOMATE it, especially with current customers."
> -- Alex Hormozi, Gym Launch Secrets

**Horseman 2: Attendance/Usage Tracking**
- Run weekly usage reports by Wednesday
- Two or fewer interactions in a week is the number-one leading indicator of cancellation
- Intercept at Week 2, not Week 5

**Horseman 3: Handwritten Cards**
- Send at sign-up (with an event invite)
- Send at referral asks (with a P.S. asking them to share)
- Send at milestones (3, 6, 12 months)
- The secret is not the card -- it is what you do AFTER you send it

> "The secret is not the card, it's what you do AFTER you send the card that matters. The card gives you the relational capital to make them feel a little guilty that they didn't send your free month to a friend."
> -- Alex Hormozi, Gym Launch Secrets

**Horseman 4: Community Events**
- Hold social events every 21 days
- Customers get in free if they bring a friend (built-in referral mechanism)
- Require new customers to attend
- Events lower churn by deepening connections between customers

**Horseman 5: Exit Interviews**
- Can save half of cancellations
- Use the CLOSER sales framework as a re-sell conversation
- Make exit interviews a requirement
- At minimum, get a phone call -- never let someone cancel via email alone

### Step 5: Deploy Internal Plays (Gym Launch Secrets -- Revenue Systems)

Internal Plays (also called "Sprinkles") are themed internal campaigns targeting your existing customer base every six weeks. They serve double duty as both retention tools and referral generators.

> "Internal plays are some of the major secrets to our success. A lack of these is usually the biggest gaping hole that I come across in gym businesses. Maybe 5% of gyms I know actually do this right."
> -- Alex Hormozi, Gym Launch Secrets

How they work:
- Create a themed campaign using one of three hook types: Nutrition-Based (e.g., "Lose While You Booze," "Eat Fat To Lose Fat"), Fitness-Based (e.g., "Big Booty Bootcamp," "6-Week 6-Pack"), or Community/Experience (e.g., Team Challenge, Battle Of The Sexes)
- Promote with a 10-day countdown of emails, videos, and texts
- Price at $150-$199, fulfill on weekends in off hours
- Expect 25% take rate from existing members at 90% margin
- Use the consultation to upsell and funnel 25-35% into higher-ticket offerings

Why they matter for referrals:
- Themed events give existing customers something exciting to invite friends to
- The bring-a-friend mechanic (Horseman 4) integrates naturally
- Customers who participate in Internal Plays are more engaged and therefore more likely to refer

### Step 6: Calculate the Pie Equation

Run the math with the user's real numbers:

1. How many new customers do you sign per month? = ___
2. What percentage of customers churn per month? = ___%
3. HGM (ceiling) = sign-ups / churn rate = ___

Show the user their current ceiling. Then show what happens when both variables improve:

| Scenario | Sign-ups | Churn | HGM (Ceiling) |
|----------|----------|-------|----------------|
| Current | ? | ?% | ? |
| Better retention only | same | half | 2x current |
| Better acquisition only | 2x | same | 2x current |
| Both improved | 2x | half | 4x current |

### Step 7: Choose and Deploy Referral Ask Methods

> "Do you know why businesses have so few referrals? They never ask for them."
> -- Alex Hormozi, $100M Leads

Seven ways to ask for referrals, from $100M Leads:

**Method 1: One-Sided Benefit**
- Pay your customer acquisition cost (CAC) directly to the referrer
- Simple and clear incentive

**Method 2: Two-Sided Benefit**
- Split CAC between the referrer and the friend
- The Dropbox/PayPal model -- both parties benefit
- Reduces the social risk of referring

**Method 3: Ask Right When They Buy**
- The moment of highest excitement is the moment of purchase
- "People who do our program with someone else tend to get 3x the results. Who else could you do this program with?"

**Method 4: Barter For Advertising**
- Trade referrals for discounts instead of just discounting
- Specific mechanics: "I will knock $100 off if you: 1) Leave reviews on all review sites, 2) Leave a video testimonial, 3) Make public social posts at beginning, middle, and end showing progress, 4) Introduce me to two friends."

> "To me, the advertising is worth more than the $100 discount. To them, the $100 is worth less than the advertising. Win-win."
> -- Alex Hormozi, $100M Money Models

**Method 5: Run Referral Events**
- Bring-a-friend events, spouse challenges, team competitions
- Customers get in free if they bring a friend
- Events create natural referral moments

**Method 6: Ongoing Referral Program**
- Always-active program with clear mechanics
- Make the referral process easy (three-way introduction, gift cards with names on them)

**Method 7: Unlockable Referral Bonuses**
- VIP status, exclusive courses, merchandise
- Gamify the referral process with tiers
- Rewards unlock as referral count increases

### Step 8: Systematize the Referral Machine

The Handwritten Card Referral Machine (Gym Launch Secrets):

1. Divide customers into four weekly groups
2. Each team member writes cards to their group each week
3. Add a P.S.: "Take a picture of this card and text it to a friend who is awesome, who you wish was here with you"
4. When the client comes in, ask: "Who did you send my card to?"
5. They probably forgot. Say: "No worries, but who WOULD you have sent it to? Go grab your phone"
6. Record a 20-second video together
7. Group-message it to the friend
8. Follow up from the team member's own phone and schedule a time

## Key Principles

> "The best source of new work is the work on your desk."
> -- Charlie Munger, quoted in $100M Leads (opening of referrals chapter)

- **Goodwill is an investment with delayed returns.** The value you give now compounds into referrals, reputation, and revenue later. Play long-term games with long-term people.

> "I am compensated tomorrow for the value I provide today."
> -- Alex Hormozi, $100M Leads

- **Referrals are always a risk for the customer.** Customers risk their own goodwill with their friend when they refer. They will only refer when they believe it is very likely their friend will have a good experience. You must make the product good enough AND make referring beneficial for both parties.

- **Never discount -- add value instead.** Do not try to build goodwill by lowering prices. Build it by increasing the gap between value delivered and price charged on the value side.

- **Lower promises, maintain quality, maximize referrals.** The fastest way to make your product remarkable is to make it better than expected. You control expectations by what you promise. Inch down promises until close rates start to dip -- this maximizes both acquisition and the goodwill that drives referrals.

- **Systematize communication, never automate it.** The moment someone realizes a piece of communication is automated, they stop paying attention. Use systems (spreadsheets, CRM, assigned groups) to ensure every customer gets genuine personal attention at scale.

- **Your customers came for the result. They stay for everything else.** What customers sign up for (the result) is what they give you money to fix. But what they come back for is everything else -- community, camaraderie, attention, and fixing problems they did not know they had. This is what Hormozi calls "abundant exchange."

- **Growing the pie beats splitting the pie.** Focus equally on reducing churn and increasing acquisition. Small improvements in both create multiplicative growth, not just additive.

- **The community happened by accident -- scale requires design.** The personal touch that worked when you knew every customer's name breaks down around 70-80 customers. Deliberate systems (the Five Horsemen) must replace what was previously accidental.

- This becomes the delivery standard for every customer

## Common Mistakes

- **Hoping for referrals instead of asking for them.** The number one reason businesses have few referrals is they never ask. Systematize the ask.

- **Asking for referrals before building goodwill.** Do not just ask for referrals without first building enough goodwill through exceptional delivery. The ask only works when customers genuinely believe their friend will have a great experience.

- **Making the referral feel one-sided.** If only the referrer benefits, the friend feels used. Use two-sided incentives (Method 2) to reduce social risk.

- **Automating customer communication.** The moment customers detect automation, they tune out. Systematize the process but keep every message personal and genuine.

- **Over-promising to close the sale.** This creates a fulfillment nightmare and zero referrals. Lower promises while maintaining quality to create room to overdeliver.

- **Focusing only on acquisition and ignoring churn.** The Pie Equation proves that reducing churn has the same mathematical impact as increasing sign-ups, often at lower cost. Most businesses obsess over getting new customers while existing ones leak out the bottom.

- **Treating retention as a passive outcome instead of an active system.** Community does not form by accident at scale. The personal touch that worked when you knew every customer's name breaks down around 70-80 customers. Deliberate systems (the Five Horsemen) must replace what was previously accidental.

- **Letting customers enter no-man's-land.** Every interaction must end with the next interaction scheduled (BAMFAM). A customer with no next appointment is a customer drifting toward churn.
