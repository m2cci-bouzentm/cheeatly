# Skill: Market Research & Demand (Starving Crowd Engine)

## When to Use

- You have an idea, skill, or product concept but have not selected a market
- Your niche feels too broad and you are competing on price
- Your offer exists but is not converting -- the market may be wrong
- You want to validate demand before investing time building

## Core Framework

### The Market Selection Hierarchy

From $100M Offers Ch.4: market selection is the single most important business decision. The hierarchy is absolute:

**Starving Crowd (market) > Offer Strength > Persuasion Skills**

A great offer in a bad market fails. A mediocre offer in a starving market prints money. Pick the market first, then build the offer.

### The Three Eternal Markets

All profitable niches trace back to one of three eternal markets ($100M Offers Ch.4):

1. **Health** -- physical fitness, mental health, longevity, appearance, energy
2. **Wealth** -- making money, saving money, investing, career advancement, business growth
3. **Relationships** -- romantic, family, professional networking, community, social status

Find a subgroup within one of these three that has all four Starving Crowd indicators.

### The Four Starving Crowd Indicators

Evaluate every potential market against these four criteria ($100M Offers Ch.4). Each is a yes-or-no qualifier -- if any indicator is absent, the market has a structural problem no offer can fix:

1. **Massive Pain** -- They desperately need what you offer. The degree of pain is proportional to the price you can charge. "The pain is the pitch" -- if you can articulate the pain more accurately than they can, they will almost always buy.
2. **Purchasing Power** -- They can afford your solution. "Solving problems for unemployed people is a terrible market." ($100M Offers)
3. **Easy to Target** -- They are gatherable through associations, mailing lists, social media groups, channels. Finding needles in a haystack kills even great offers.
4. **Growing** -- Growing markets are tailwinds. Declining markets are headwinds. "Grow or Die -- maintenance is a myth. The market grows at 9% per year minimum. If you are not growing, you are falling behind." ($100M Offers)

### Niche Pricing Escalation

From $100M Offers Ch.4: the same product can command 100x the price when targeted to a specific avatar. Each level of specificity allows a higher price because the prospect thinks "this was made exactly for me."

**Example 1 -- Time Management:**
- "Time Management" -- $19
- "Time Management for Sales Professionals" -- $99
- "Time Management for B2B Outbound Sales Reps" -- $499
- "Time Management for B2B Outbound Power Tools Sales Reps" -- $2,000

**Example 2 -- Fitness:**
- "A fitness program for weight loss" -- $19
- "A fitness program for shift-nurses who need to lose weight" -- $1,997

The core content is nearly identical. The specificity commands the premium.

> "You want to be 'the guy' who services 'this type of person' or solves 'this type of problem.' And even more niched: 'I solve this type of problem for this specific type of person in this unique counter-intuitive way that reverses their deepest fear.'" -- $100M Offers

### The Niche Slap Principle

From $100M Offers Ch.4: Do not hop from niche to niche thinking the market is the problem. The real problem is you have not committed long enough to find your Grand Slam Offer.

> "Don't make me niche slap you."

All markets have unpleasant characteristics. The grass is never greener. The instinct when something is not working is to change markets -- the correct move is usually to change the offer within the same market.

> "Pick one and commit until you figure it out." -- $100M Offers

> "If your offer doesn't work, it doesn't mean you suck. It means your offer sucks. Big difference. You only suck if you stop trying." -- $100M Offers, Section V

**Commitment rule:** "Building something great takes 5-10 years even when you know exactly what to do." ($100M Leads). At minimum, commit to your niche long enough to exhaust your offer iterations before considering a market switch.

## Step-by-Step Process

### STEP 1: Identify Starting Point

Ask the user:
- What problem do you solve or what skill/expertise do you have?
- Which of the three eternal markets does it touch? (Health, Wealth, or Relationships)
- Who currently has this problem the worst?

### STEP 2: Generate Micro-Niches

Break the broad market into progressively specific subgroups using the Niche Pricing Escalation principle.

For each idea, generate at least 3 levels of niching:
- Level 1: Broad category (e.g., "fitness")
- Level 2: Specific demographic (e.g., "fitness for busy dads over 35")
- Level 3: Hyper-specific avatar (e.g., "fitness for tech executives who travel 3+ days per week and have gained 20+ pounds since their promotion")

Generate at least 3 micro-niche candidates at Level 2-3 depth.

### STEP 3: Evaluate Each Niche Against the Four Indicators

For each micro-niche, evaluate against the four Starving Crowd indicators as qualitative yes/no checks:

| Indicator | Present? (Yes/No) | Evidence |
|-----------|-------------------|----------|
| Massive Pain | | Do they desperately need this? How intense and frequent is the pain? |
| Purchasing Power | | Can they afford $500+? $2,000+? Are they employed in roles with disposable income? |
| Easy to Target | | Where do they gather? Can you reach 1,000 of them this week? Are there associations, lists, groups? |
| Growing | | Is this market expanding or contracting? Is the industry growing year over year? |

**Disqualify any niche missing even one indicator.** A niche without pain will not buy. A niche without purchasing power cannot buy. A niche you cannot reach will never see your offer. A declining niche means fighting headwinds.

### STEP 4: Select the Best Niche

Choose the niche with:
- All four indicators clearly present
- Strongest pain + purchasing power combination (these two matter most)
- Clear targeting channels you can access today

If two niches both pass all four indicators, choose the one where you have the strongest evidence of existing demand -- people already spending money on solutions.

### STEP 5: Extract Customer Pain

For the selected niche, use the Problem-Solution Exhaustion Method from $100M Offers Ch.9 to systematically extract pain:

1. Write down the dream outcome for the niche
2. List every single thing the prospect must do to achieve it
3. For each thing, write down every reason they would fail -- mapping each to the four value drivers (financial worth, likelihood of success, effort/sacrifice, time)
4. You should end up with 32-64 sub-problems

Extract real language: how do customers actually describe this problem? Use their informal phrasing, not clinical language. "The point of good persuasion is for the prospect to feel understood, not for them to understand you." ($100M Offers)

Look for evidence of pain in:
- Forum posts, Reddit threads, Facebook groups
- Amazon reviews of competing products (complaints reveal unmet needs)
- Questions asked in communities and comment sections
- Support tickets and complaints about existing solutions

### STEP 6: Classify Demand Signals

Split the audience using the $100M Leads distinction between leads and engaged leads:

**Engaged Leads** -- people who have shown interest in the stuff you sell. They actively search, compare, and spend money on solutions. These are your primary targets. ($100M Leads: "An engaged lead is a person who has shown interest in the stuff you sell.")

**Leads** -- people you can contact but who have not shown interest. They may be in your target demographic but have not taken action. These are secondary targets. ($100M Leads: "A lead is just a person you can contact.")

Look for demand evidence:
- Existing competitors with customers (competitors prove the market exists)
- People asking questions in forums, groups, communities
- Paid solutions already in market (people spending money is the strongest signal)
- Repeated complaints about existing solutions (gap in the market)

### STEP 7: Validate Demand

Before building a full product, validate with real market signals using these Hormozi-sourced approaches:

**The First Five Free Strategy** ($100M Leads) -- Offer your first five customers the product for free in exchange for feedback, testimonials, and referrals. Then increase price by 20% every five customers: 80% off, 60% off, 40% off, etc. "You probably suck (for now). People are far more forgiving when you haven't charged anything." This tests whether people will use and value your solution even when price is not a barrier.

**Lead Magnet Test** ($100M Leads) -- Create a lead magnet: "a complete solution to a narrow problem that, once solved, reveals another problem solved by your core offer." Use the 7-step framework:
1. Pick a narrow problem to solve for the right customer
2. Choose how to solve it (reveal problems, samples/trials, or one step of a multi-step process)
3. Choose delivery method (software, information, services, or physical products)
4. Test the name (headline, image, subheadline)
5. Make it easy to consume
6. Make it genuinely good -- "give away the secrets, sell the implementation" ($100M Leads)
7. Clear CTA with reasons to act now

If people consume your lead magnet eagerly and ask what else you offer, demand exists. If nobody engages even with free material, the problem is not price -- it is that the hidden costs (time, effort, sacrifice) outweigh the perceived value. ($100M Leads: "If you struggle to give your stuff away for free, the problem is not price -- it is the hidden costs.")

**Pre-Sell Test** -- Offer the solution before building it. Gauge willingness to pay with real money, not "I would definitely buy that." Define success criteria before running the test. "10 people said they are interested" is not validation. "4 people paid a deposit" is.

## Key Principles

- **"A starving crowd beats everything."** -- The single most important factor in business success is market selection, not offer quality or sales skill. ($100M Offers Ch.4)
- **"The pain is the pitch."** -- If you can articulate the pain a prospect is feeling more accurately than they can, they will almost always buy. Spend most of your research understanding pain, not designing solutions. ($100M Offers)
- **"Grow or Die -- maintenance is a myth."** -- The market grows at 9% per year minimum. A plateau is not stability; it is decline relative to the market. Only enter growing markets. ($100M Offers)
- **"Don't make me niche slap you."** -- Commit to your niche. The instinct when things are not working is to change markets. The correct move is to change the offer within the same market. ($100M Offers Ch.4)
- **"Pick one and commit until you figure it out."** -- All markets have unpleasant characteristics. The grass is never greener. ($100M Offers)
- **Niche Pricing Escalation** -- The same product can be priced 100x higher through specificity. A generic course is $19; the same course for a hyper-specific avatar is $2,000. ($100M Offers Ch.4)
- **Market > Offer > Persuasion** -- This hierarchy is absolute. No amount of persuasion skill fixes a bad market. No offer quality compensates for a market without pain or money. ($100M Offers Ch.4)
- **Fear of loss is stronger than desire for gain.** -- When evaluating markets, look for people who are losing something (health, money, relationships) rather than people who want a nice-to-have improvement. Loss-driven markets convert faster. ($100M Offers Ch.12)
- **"Give away the secrets, sell the implementation."** -- Your free stuff should be so good people feel obligated to pay you. Use this principle when testing demand: if your free content generates demand, the paid version will sell. ($100M Leads)

## Output Structure

The skill produces a single document: **MARKET_RESEARCH.md**

```md
# MARKET_RESEARCH.md

## 1. Eternal Market
- Which of the three: Health / Wealth / Relationships
- Sub-category within the eternal market

## 2. Micro-Niches Evaluated
| Niche | Massive Pain? | Purchasing Power? | Easy to Target? | Growing? | Pass/Fail |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|-----------------|----------|-----------|
| Niche 1 | Yes/No | Yes/No | Yes/No | Yes/No | |
| Niche 2 | Yes/No | Yes/No | Yes/No | Yes/No | |
| Niche 3 | Yes/No | Yes/No | Yes/No | Yes/No | |

## 3. Selected Niche
- Final choice
- Why this niche wins (strongest pain + purchasing power + clear targeting)
- Niche Pricing Escalation path (Level 1 -> Level 2 -> Level 3 with estimated price at each level)

## 4. Customer Pain Map (Problem-Solution Exhaustion)
- Dream outcome for this niche
- Everything they must do to achieve it
- Every reason they would fail (mapped to value drivers: financial, likelihood, effort, time)
- Raw language (how they actually describe these problems)

## 5. Demand Signals
- Existing competitors and what they charge
- Evidence of active searching/buying
- Gaps in current solutions
- Engaged leads vs leads classification (who has shown interest vs who can merely be contacted)

## 6. Validation Plan
- First Five Free: who are the five people, what will you offer, what feedback/testimonials will you request
- Lead Magnet: what narrow problem will it solve, what format, what CTA
- Pre-Sell (optional): what will you offer, success criteria
- Success criteria (specific, measurable)

## 7. Niche Commitment
- Commitment statement: what niche you are committing to
- What you will change if it does not work (the offer, NOT the market)
```

## Common Mistakes

- **Switching niches instead of improving the offer.** The Niche Slap principle exists because this is the most common entrepreneurial mistake. When conversions are low, fix the offer, the messaging, or the delivery -- not the market. "Pick one and commit until you figure it out." ($100M Offers Ch.4)
- **Targeting people without purchasing power.** "Solving problems for unemployed people is a terrible market." Purchasing power is a hard requirement -- no amount of pain compensates for an audience that cannot pay. ($100M Offers Ch.4)
- **Staying too broad to "not miss anyone."** Broad markets force you to compete on price. The Niche Pricing Escalation principle proves that specificity commands premium pricing. A fitness program for weight loss is $19; a fitness program for shift-nurses who need to lose weight is $1,997. You make more money serving fewer people at higher prices. ($100M Offers Ch.4)
- **Ignoring market growth trajectory.** Entering a declining market means fighting headwinds no matter how good your offer is. The newspaper industry example -- same entrepreneur, different market, wildly different results. ($100M Offers Ch.4)
- **Giving up too early and personalizing failure.** "If your offer doesn't work, it doesn't mean you suck. It means your offer sucks. Big difference. You only suck if you stop trying. If you try one hundred offers, you will succeed." ($100M Offers, Section V)
