## When to Use

- The offer feels weak, price resistance is high, or conversions are low
- You need to increase perceived value without discounting
- Objections are blocking sales and you want to neutralize them with tangible assets

## Core Framework

Bonuses come from Chapter 14 of *$100M Offers*. The foundational rule:

> "Never discount the main offer. It teaches your customers that your prices are negotiable. Adding bonuses to increase value to close the deal is far superior to cutting prices."

The core insight is that **a single offer is less valuable than the same offer broken into components and stacked as bonuses** ($100M Offers, Ch.14). The same fulfillment, repackaged as a core offer plus named bonuses with assigned dollar values, is perceived as dramatically more valuable than the identical thing sold as one unit.

This works because of the Value Equation ($100M Offers, Ch.6):

```
Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice)
```

Each bonus should move one of these four drivers -- increasing dream outcome or perceived likelihood, or decreasing time delay or effort. Bonuses that reduce the bottom of the equation (effort, time) are especially powerful because, as Hormozi notes, "beginner marketers focus on bigger claims (top of equation). Advanced marketers focus on reducing time delay and effort (bottom of equation)."

**Strategic principle -- bonuses create upsell opportunities:** Every bonus you give solves a problem but simultaneously reveals a new one. The problem-solution cycle means every bonus is an upsell opportunity waiting to happen ($100M Money Models). Give away free ear muffs, then charge $30 to store them. Include free supplements in a nutrition plan, then sell more supplements. Give free onboarding, then charge for advanced training. Design each bonus with awareness of what new problem it creates and what you can sell to solve it.

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Identify Key Objections

List every reason a prospect would say no. Each bonus must map to a specific objection -- bonuses without a matching objection are dead weight.

Common objection categories mapped to Value Equation drivers:

| Objection | Value Driver |
|-----------|-------------|
| "I don't have time" | Time Delay |
| "It's too complicated" | Effort & Sacrifice |
| "This won't work for me" | Perceived Likelihood |
| "I don't know if the result is worth it" | Dream Outcome |
| "I might fail" | Perceived Likelihood |
| "It's too expensive" | Dream Outcome (value gap) |

If objections are not known, infer from the audience, offer type, and price point. A single unresolved objection can kill a sale (Ch.9: "You must resolve every obstacle a buyer believes they will have").

### Step 2: Map Each Objection to a Solution

For each objection, define what removes it:

- "I don't have time" --> reduce effort or speed up the process
- "I don't know how" --> provide guidance, templates, or tools
- "I might fail" --> add support, accountability, or a guarantee
- "It's too complicated" --> provide checklists, done-for-you setup, or step-by-step systems

### Step 3: Convert Solutions into Named Bonuses

Turn each solution into a tangible, named asset. Every bonus name should communicate the benefit in the title itself (Ch.14):

> "Instead of 'Meal Plan,' name it 'Personalized Lick-Your-Fingers-Good Meal Plan -- so good it will be easier to follow than what you used to cheat with ($500 value).'"

**Use the Splinter Stack technique** (Gym Launch Secrets): Unbundle everything you already do and list each item separately to make the offer feel massive. Rather than quoting one lump number, walk through each component of your service separately. The same fulfillment feels dramatically more valuable when each piece is named and valued independently.

How to Splinter Stack:
1. List everything included in your current fulfillment
2. Separate each component into its own named bonus
3. Assign a standalone dollar value to each component
4. Present them as individual items, not a single bundle

**Apply the M-A-G-I-C Naming Formula** ($100M Offers, Ch.16) to each bonus name:
- **M** - Make a Magnetic Reason Why (Free, Grand Opening, New Year)
- **A** - Announce Your Avatar (who specifically this is for)
- **G** - Give Them A Goal (the dream outcome)
- **I** - Indicate a Time Interval (duration sets expectations)
- **C** - Complete With A Container Word (Blueprint, Checklist, System, Accelerator, Sprint)

Use 3-5 components. The shorter and punchier the better. Rhyme and alliterate when possible but do not force it.

**Tools and checklists outperform additional trainings as bonus formats.** People do not want more information -- they want less effort. This reduces the bottom of the Value Equation ($100M Offers, Ch.6 and Ch.14). Prioritize these formats:

- Checklists and templates (lowest effort for the user)
- Tools and calculators (automate decisions)
- Swipe files and scripts (eliminate blank-page syndrome)
- Done-for-you setups (zero effort)
- Audits and assessments (personalized certainty)

Avoid defaulting to "bonus training modules" or "extra video content" -- these increase the user's effort and time, which moves the bottom of the Value Equation in the wrong direction.

### Step 4: Apply Partner Bonus Arbitrage (Zero-Cost Bonuses)

From Ch.14 -- get adjacent, non-competing businesses to provide their products or services as bonuses in exchange for exposure to your customer base:

> "This is free marketing for them, and high value products for you at no cost."

How it works:
1. Identify businesses whose customers overlap with yours but who do not compete
2. Offer them exposure to your buyer list in exchange for their product/service as a bonus
3. Negotiate group discounts AND referral commissions on top

Example from the book: A pain clinic got free massages, free chiropractic adjustments, supplement discounts, and gym memberships -- all as bonuses. Total bonus value exceeded the $400 offer price. Each partner also paid referral commissions, creating $350 additional profit per client.

Partner bonuses are the highest-leverage play because they cost you nothing, increase perceived value, and can become revenue streams.

### Step 5: Ensure Bonus Value Eclipses Core Offer Value

The value of bonuses should eclipse the core offer value ($100M Offers, Ch.14). This is not optional -- it is the mechanism that makes the prospect think "I'd be stupid not to take this."

For each bonus, assign a perceived value based on:
- The outcome it delivers
- The time it saves
- What it would cost to get this separately
- The difficulty of doing it without the bonus

Then sum all bonus values and verify the total exceeds the core offer value.

### Step 6: Plan the Presentation Sequence

**This is the most commonly missed technique.** From Ch.14, bonuses are presented AFTER the first ask, not before:

> In 1-on-1 selling: ask for the sale first. If they say yes, the bonuses become a wow moment that reinforces their decision and eliminates buyer's remorse. If they say no, match each bonus to their specific objection, then ask again.

The sequence:
1. Present the core offer with its full value
2. Ask for the sale
3. If YES: reveal bonuses as a surprise ("And you also get...") -- creates delight and reinforces the decision
4. If NO: identify the specific objection, present the bonus that addresses it ("I totally understand. That is exactly why we created [Bonus Name]..."), then re-ask

> "People have a hard time rejecting someone who keeps accommodating their concerns."

Each added bonus creates reciprocity pressure. The prospect must keep saying no to someone who keeps giving them more.

**Surprise and Delight** ($100M Money Models): If a prospect says yes before you have presented all bonuses, still give them all of them.

> "If they say yes before you add them, still give them all four. It will surprise and delight them."

This guarantees equal treatment across all customers, builds massive goodwill, and creates strong conditions for future upsells.

For sales pages and 1-to-many selling, stack all bonuses after the core offer presentation but before the price reveal.

### Step 7: Trim Low-Value Bonuses

Too many bonuses dilute clarity. Apply the Trim and Stack principle from Ch.10: remove high-cost, low-value items and keep low-cost, high-value items.

Remove a bonus if:
- It does not map to a specific objection
- It increases effort or time for the user (wrong direction on the Value Equation)
- It overlaps with another bonus
- Its perceived value is low relative to the others

## Key Principles

- **Never Discount -- Add Bonuses Instead**: "Discounting your main offer teaches customers that your prices are negotiable and puts you in a position of weakness. Adding bonuses increases perceived value while maintaining your price integrity and positioning you in a stance of goodwill and strength." ($100M Offers, Ch.14)

- **A Single Offer Broken Into Components Is More Valuable**: The same fulfillment repackaged as core offer plus named bonuses with dollar values is perceived as dramatically more valuable than the identical thing sold as one unit. ($100M Offers, Ch.14)

- **Each Bonus Must Address a Specific Objection**: Bonuses without a matching objection are clutter. Every bonus in the stack should have a clear answer to "which objection does this kill?" ($100M Offers, Ch.14)

- **Tools and Checklists Beat Trainings**: Bonus formats that reduce effort (tools, checklists, templates, done-for-you) outperform formats that add effort (courses, trainings, extra content). The goal is to decrease the bottom of the Value Equation. ($100M Offers, Ch.14 and Ch.6)

- **Ask First, Reveal Bonuses After**: In 1-on-1 selling, always ask for the sale before presenting bonuses. Yes = wow moment. No = match bonus to objection, re-ask. ($100M Offers, Ch.14)

- **Surprise and Delight**: If they say yes before all bonuses are revealed, give them all anyway. Builds goodwill and sets up future upsells. ($100M Money Models)

- **Partner Bonus Arbitrage**: Get adjacent businesses to provide bonuses for free in exchange for exposure to your customer base. Zero cost to you, high perceived value to the buyer. ($100M Offers, Ch.14)

- **Bonus Value Should Eclipse Core Offer Value**: The total perceived value of all bonuses combined should exceed the core offer value. This creates the "I'd be stupid not to take this" reaction. ($100M Offers, Ch.14)

- **Splinter Stack Your Offer**: Unbundle everything you already do and list each item separately to make the offer feel massive. Walk through each component separately rather than quoting one lump number. (Gym Launch Secrets)

- **Bonuses Create Upsell Problems**: Every bonus solves a problem but reveals a new one. Design bonuses with awareness of the next problem they create -- that problem is your next offer opportunity. ($100M Money Models)

- **Name Everything With M-A-G-I-C**: Use the five-component naming formula -- Magnetic reason why, Avatar, Goal, Interval, Container word -- to name each bonus so it communicates value in the title itself. ($100M Offers, Ch.16)

## Common Mistakes

- **Discounting instead of adding bonuses**: Never lower the price. Add value. "Any fool can sell a product by offering it for a discount. It takes great marketing to sell the same product for a premium." (Rory Sutherland, quoted in $100M Offers Ch.6)

- **Dumping all bonuses in the initial pitch**: In 1-on-1 selling, this wastes the psychological leverage of matching specific bonuses to specific objections after the first ask.

- **Adding training content as bonuses**: More videos and courses increase the buyer's effort and time delay. Use tools, checklists, and templates that decrease effort instead.

- **Bonuses that don't map to objections**: Every bonus must kill a specific reason to say no. If you cannot name the objection it addresses, remove it.

- **Generic bonus names**: "Bonus videos" or "Extra resources" communicate nothing. Name each bonus with the M-A-G-I-C formula and assign a dollar value.

- **Bonus value lower than core offer**: If the bonus stack does not eclipse the core offer value, the "I'd be stupid not to take this" reaction will not trigger.

- **Ignoring partner arbitrage**: Most businesses never ask adjacent businesses for free bonuses. This is the highest-leverage, zero-cost technique available.

- **Not planning the upsell problem**: Each bonus solves a problem and reveals a new one. If you do not design for this cycle, you leave money on the table.

- **Presenting the offer as one lump sum**: Use the Splinter Stack -- unbundle and list each component separately to make the offer feel massive.
