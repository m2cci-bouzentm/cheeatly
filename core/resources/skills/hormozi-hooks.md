## When to Use

- Content is not getting attention, clicks, or engagement and needs stronger hooks
- Writing ads (paid or organic) and need systematic angle generation, not random brainstorming
- Writing any copy (landing pages, emails, ads) and need a grounded copywriting checklist
- Existing hooks or ads have fatigued and need refreshing using the correct change order

## Core Framework: The Content Unit (Hook-Retain-Reward)

From $100M Leads: all audience-growing content does one thing -- rewards the people consuming it. Every piece of content is built from one atomic structure.

```
CONTENT UNIT = Hook + Retain + Reward
```

**Hook** -- Get them to notice. Use interesting topics, strong headlines, and platform-matched formatting. "Five times more people read your headline than anything else" (David Ogilvy, cited in $100M Leads). "When you have written your headline, you have spent 80 cents of your advertising dollar."

**Retain** -- Keep them consuming. Use lists, steps, and stories to embed unresolved questions. Short content hooks/retains/rewards fewer times; long content does it more times. Longer content takes more skill because you string more good content units in a row.

To strengthen the Retain section, use the **Belief-Breaking Formula** (from Gym Launch Secrets, attributed to Linh Trinh) to hold attention by shattering false beliefs mid-content:

1. Say what they believe ("So you believe cheap front-end offers work?")
2. Say why it is wrong ("This does not provide cash flow to market or acquire customers")
3. Say what is right ("Use a high-ticket front-end acquisition")
4. Say WHY it is right ("It will allow you to spend whatever you need to get people in profitably")
5. Show proof -- third-party stats, testimonials, wall of results

This formula creates a belief gap that keeps the audience engaged through the content to see the resolution. It works in written content, video, and presentations.

(Gym Launch Secrets, "The Belief-Breaking Formula")

**Reward** -- Satisfy the reason they consumed it. Match or exceed the expectations set by the hook. "There is no such thing as too long, only too boring." Streaming platforms prove people will binge for hours. Our biology has not changed; people just have more rewarding alternatives.

The content you create is not the compounding asset -- the audience is. When Hormozi put out 10x the content, his audience grew 10x faster.

($100M Leads, Ch. "Post Free Content Part I")

## The Three Structural Components of Every Ad

From $100M Leads Ch. "Run Paid Ads Part I: Making An Ad": every ad has exactly three elements.

1. **Call Outs** -- Get noticed. Use the Cocktail Party Effect (see below) to make the right person's head turn. "If they never notice your ad, nothing else matters."

2. **Value Elements** -- Get interested. Use the What-Who-When framework (see below) to show why they should care. This is the body of the ad.

3. **CTA** -- Tell them what to do next. Specific action, clear next step.

"My advertising became 20x more effective when I focused the majority of my effort on the first five seconds. The purpose of each second of the ad is to sell the next second."

($100M Leads, Ch. "Run Paid Ads Part I")

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Identify Your Inputs

Gather:
- **WHO**: The specific audience (the more specific, the better -- Niche Pricing Escalation applies to hooks too)
- **RESULT**: The dream outcome they want
- **PAIN**: What they are currently struggling with
- **PROOF**: What you or your clients have achieved

Use "How I" framing, not "How To." This is one of the few data-backed headline optimizations in the extractions: Hormozi tested "How to get strangers to want to buy your stuff" against "Get strangers to want to buy your stuff." Adding just "how to" overwhelmingly won. And separately, framing as personal experience ("How I") beats prescriptive ("How To") because "When you tell your experience, you are sharing value. When you tell a stranger what to do, it's hard to avoid coming off preachy or arrogant." Examples: "How I Built My 7-Figure Agency" (not "How To Build a 7-Figure Agency"). "My favorite way to generate leads" (not "This is the best way to generate leads").

($100M Leads, "Switch From How To to How I" and "Test Ad Headlines Relentlessly")

### Step 2: Generate Call Outs Using the Cocktail Party Effect

From $100M Leads: at a noisy party, you hear your name across the room because your brain filters for personally relevant information. Ads must trigger the same effect. Four types of verbal callouts:

1. **Labels**: Call out the audience by name.
   - "Clark County Moms"
   - "Gym Owners"
   - "LOCAL AREA + TYPE OF PERSON is still one of my all-time favorite ways to get attention. Chino Hills Residents who want to lose weight."

2. **Yes-Questions**: Ask a question only your ideal prospect answers yes to.
   - "Do you wake up to pee more than once?"
   - "Are you spending more than $100K/month on ads?"

3. **If-Then Statements**: Condition + specific benefit.
   - "If you run over $100K/month in ads, we can save you 20%"

4. **Ridiculous Results**: A result so extreme it demands attention.
   - "Massage studio books out two years in advance"

Combine verbal callouts with **nonverbal callouts**: contrast (something visually unexpected), likeness (someone who looks like the prospect), scene-setting (environment the prospect recognizes).

($100M Leads, "Cocktail Party Effect Call Outs")

### Step 3: Build Value Elements Using the What-Who-When Framework

From $100M Leads: this framework generates dozens of unique advertising angles by combining three dimensions.

**WHAT (8 Value Elements)**:
1. Dream Outcome -- the positive result
2. Nightmare -- the negative result (opposite)
3. Perceived Likelihood -- why they will succeed
4. Risk -- why they might fail (opposite)
5. Speed -- how fast they get results
6. Time Delay -- how long it painfully takes without you (opposite)
7. Ease -- how little effort is required
8. Effort/Sacrifice -- how hard their current approach is (opposite)

**WHO (Stakeholder Perspectives)**:
- The customer themselves
- Their spouse
- Their kids
- Their parents
- Their colleagues
- Their boss
- Their friends
- Their rivals/competitors

"People are primarily status driven -- show how your product changes how others treat your customer."

**WHEN (3 Time Horizons)**:
- Past -- what happened before, what they tried, what failed
- Present -- what is happening right now, the current pain
- Future -- what will happen if they act (or do not act)

**Combining all three creates dozens of unique advertising angles.** Short ads use fewer angles, long ads use more -- but callouts and CTAs stay the same.

Example angle generation:
- Dream Outcome + Boss + Future = "Imagine your boss saying 'I do not know what changed, but keep doing it'"
- Nightmare + Spouse + Present = "Your spouse is watching you stress about [problem] every night"
- Ease + Rivals + Past = "While your competitors were grinding, our clients were scaling on autopilot"

($100M Leads, "What-Who-When Ad Framework")

### Step 4: Apply the 7 Headline Attention Triggers

From $100M Leads, Content Unit framework: headlines that work use one or more of these triggers:

1. **Recency** -- what is happening right now
2. **Relevancy** -- directly about their situation
3. **Celebrity** -- involves someone they know or admire
4. **Proximity** -- happening near them (geographically or socially)
5. **Conflict** -- tension, disagreement, controversy
6. **Unusual** -- something unexpected or counterintuitive
7. **Ongoing** -- a developing story or series they want to follow

Test headlines relentlessly. The "how to" A/B test result bears repeating: Hormozi tested "How to get strangers to want to buy your stuff" against "Get strangers to want to buy your stuff." Adding just "how to" overwhelmingly won. Small word changes make massive differences -- test headlines first, then images, then subheadlines.

($100M Leads, "Post Free Content" and "Test Ad Headlines Relentlessly")

### Step 5: Apply the Twelve Copy Commandments

From Gym Launch Secrets: these twelve rules govern all copy -- ads, landing pages, emails, sales pages. Apply them to every piece of content the skill produces.

1. **Headline comes first** -- curiosity is king. Five times more people read your headline than anything else. This is the single highest-leverage element.

2. **Say what only you can say** -- unique positioning. If a competitor could run the same ad with their name swapped in, the copy is not specific enough.

3. **Call out who you are looking for AND who you are NOT** -- repelling the wrong audience is as important as attracting the right one. Specificity is the mechanism.

4. **Reason why -- use the word "because"** -- people comply with requests at dramatically higher rates when given a reason, even a weak one.

5. **Damaging admission** -- own your flaws for authenticity. Admitting a weakness builds trust and makes every other claim more believable.

6. **Show, do not tell** -- describe emotions and scenarios rather than listing features. Example from Gym Launch Secrets: Instead of "make more sales," write: "You will be amazed when you hear customers read their credit card numbers to you over the phone after reading two magic sentences. It will feel like taking orders."

7. **Tie benefits to status** -- frame outcomes in terms of how others will perceive the prospect's achievement. Status is the meta-driver of all dream outcomes.

8. **Use urgency and scarcity legitimately** -- real deadlines, real limits. Fake scarcity destroys trust.

9. **Implied authority** -- mention credentials, numbers, notable clients. Do not brag; let facts imply expertise.

10. **P.S. = Power Sentence** -- the P.S. is the second most read part of any written piece after the headline. Use it for a final compelling point or CTA.

11. **Clear next steps** -- Step 1, Step 2, Step 3. Remove ambiguity about what to do next.

12. **Third-grade reading level** -- "Fancy equals friction. Simple equals sales. I put every ad I have through a free reading-level tool and continue to edit the copy until it is at a third-grade reading level or below." (Gym Launch Secrets)

(Gym Launch Secrets, "The Twelve Copy Commandments")

### Step 6: Assemble the Full Ad or Content Piece

Structure every piece using the three components:

```
[CALL OUT] -- Cocktail Party Effect callout (Label, Yes-Question, If-Then, or Ridiculous Result)
     |
[VALUE ELEMENT(S)] -- What-Who-When angles (use 1-3 for short content, more for long)
     |
[CTA] -- Specific action with reason to act now
```

Run the assembled piece through the Twelve Copy Commandments as a checklist before publishing.

For content (not ads), follow **Give Until They Ask**: "The moment you start asking for money is the moment you decide to slow down growth. The more patient you are, the more you will get when you finally ask." Give in public, ask in private. Let the audience self-select when ready.

($100M Leads, "Give Until They Ask")

### Step 7: Optimize Using More Better New

When you need more results from your hooks and ads, apply the three levers in order:

```
More (volume) -> Better (test constraints) -> New (new placements -> new platforms -> new activities)
```

1. **More**: Do more of what works. "Most businesses have NOT saturated their market -- they have saturated their effort." What is stopping you from doing 10x what you are currently doing?

2. **Better**: Find the biggest constraint (the biggest drop-off point) and test one thing per week per platform.

3. **New**: Try new placements on current platform, then new platforms, then entirely new Core Four activities.

Always in that order. Most people skip to "New" when they have not exhausted "More."

($100M Leads, "Core Four On Steroids: More Better New")

### Step 8: When Ads Fatigue, Follow the Change Order

From $100M Offers Ch.16: when an offer or ad stops performing, change elements in this specific order. Each step is cheaper and less risky than the next:

```
1. Creative (images, video, visuals)        -- cheapest change
2. Body copy                                 -- low cost
3. Headline / wrapper / name                 -- medium cost
4. Duration / time interval                  -- medium cost
5. Enhancer / discount                       -- higher cost
6. Monetization structure                    -- LAST RESORT
```

"You are not changing the actual offer -- only the wrapping paper. This one concept will get you leads forever. The same core offer with a new name can get 2x, 3x, or 10x the response rate."

The fulfillment never changes. Only the packaging.

($100M Offers, Ch.16 "Naming" -- Seasonal Offer Wrapping)

## Key Principles

- **"Five times more people read your headline than anything else."** -- David Ogilvy, cited in $100M Leads. Headlines are 80% of the advertising dollar. Test them first, test them most.

- **"The purpose of each second of the ad is to sell the next second."** Focus the majority of effort on the first five seconds. ($100M Leads, Ch. "Run Paid Ads Part I")

- **"There is no such thing as too long, only too boring."** People will consume hours of content if it rewards them consistently. The issue is never length -- it is quality. ($100M Leads)

- **"Fancy equals friction. Simple equals sales."** Write at a third-grade reading level. Put every ad through a reading-level tool. (Gym Launch Secrets, Twelve Copy Commandments)

- **Give Until They Ask.** Hormozi's evolution of Vaynerchuk's "jab jab jab right hook." Growing platforms dramatically over-give. "Give away the secrets. Sell the implementation." ($100M Leads)

- **"How I" beats "How To."** Talk about what you have done, not what people should do. "When you tell your experience, you are sharing value." This is backed by A/B test data: adding "how to" to a headline overwhelmingly won against the version without it. ($100M Leads)

- **Puddles Ponds Lakes Oceans.** Start with the narrowest possible topic (puddle) where you can be the undisputed expert. Expand as authority grows. Do not compete with people who have better track records in broad topics. ($100M Leads)

- **Status is the meta-driver.** All dream outcomes are ultimately about status -- perceived increase or decrease in relative standing. "Even rejecting a Lamborghini for a minivan is a status play." Frame benefits in terms of how others will perceive the prospect's achievement. ($100M Offers, Ch.6)

- **Damaging admission builds trust.** Owning your flaws makes every other claim more believable. (Gym Launch Secrets, Twelve Copy Commandments #5)

- **Show, do not tell.** Describe emotions and scenarios rather than listing features. (Gym Launch Secrets, Twelve Copy Commandments #6)

## Output Structure

The skill produces:

### 1. Call Outs (3-5 variations)
Using the four Cocktail Party Effect types:
- Label callouts
- Yes-Question callouts
- If-Then callouts
- Ridiculous Result callouts

### 2. Value Element Angles (8-15 variations)
Using the What-Who-When framework:
- At least one angle per value element (Dream Outcome, Nightmare, Likelihood, Risk, Speed, Delay, Ease, Effort)
- Mixed across stakeholder perspectives (self, spouse, boss, rivals, etc.)
- Mixed across time horizons (past, present, future)

### 3. Full Content Units (3-5 variations)
Each containing:
- Hook (callout + headline trigger)
- Retain structure (lists, steps, story, or Belief-Breaking Formula sequence)
- Reward (the payoff that matches the hook's promise)

### 4. Complete Ads (3 variations)
Each structured as:
- Call Out
- Value Element(s)
- CTA

Each ad checked against the Twelve Copy Commandments.

### 5. Fatigue Rotation Plan
What to change first, second, third when performance drops, following the six-step change order.

## Common Mistakes

- **Writing hooks without the full Content Unit.** Hooks are only one-third of the structure. A great hook with no retain or reward kills trust and future engagement. ($100M Leads, Content Unit framework)

- **Random brainstorming instead of systematic angle generation.** The What-Who-When framework produces dozens of angles methodically. Random ideation misses most of them. ($100M Leads)

- **Skipping the callout.** If the right person never notices the ad, nothing else matters. The Cocktail Party Effect must come first. ($100M Leads)

- **Changing the offer when the ad fatigues.** The offer and fulfillment never change. Change the wrapping in the correct order: creative, body, headline, duration, enhancer, monetization (last resort). ($100M Offers, Ch.16)

- **Being preachy instead of sharing.** Use "How I" not "How To." Talk about experience, not prescriptions. ($100M Leads)

- **Jumping to "New" before exhausting "More" and "Better."** Most businesses have not saturated their effort, not their market. Do more of what works first. ($100M Leads, More Better New)

- **Ignoring status framing.** Generic benefit language underperforms status-framed language. "How others will perceive the prospect's achievement" is more motivating than raw feature descriptions. ($100M Offers, Ch.6)

- **Starting too broad.** Puddles first, then ponds, then lakes, then oceans. Start with the narrowest topic where you can be the undisputed expert. ($100M Leads)

- **Writing above a third-grade reading level.** "Fancy equals friction. Simple equals sales." Run every piece of copy through a reading-level tool. (Gym Launch Secrets, Twelve Copy Commandments #12)

- **Telling instead of showing.** Listing features instead of describing emotions and scenarios. "You will be amazed when you hear customers read their credit card numbers to you over the phone" beats "increase sales." (Gym Launch Secrets, Twelve Copy Commandments #6)
