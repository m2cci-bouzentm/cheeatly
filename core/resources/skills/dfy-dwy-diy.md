## When to Use
- The offer exists but delivery format is unclear, weak, or mismatched to the customer
- Conversions are low despite a strong outcome promise -- the delivery model may be the bottleneck
- Scaling vs. customization is a tradeoff and the user needs to decide how to deliver value at different price points

## Core Framework

### The Value Equation Connection

Every delivery decision maps to the Value Equation from $100M Offers Ch.6:

```
Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice)
```

DFY delivery drives the bottom of the equation toward zero. When Effort & Sacrifice approaches zero, perceived value approaches infinity. "DFY services cost more than DIY because effort is eliminated. In an ideal world, the prospect says yes and the outcome happens with zero additional effort" ($100M Offers Ch.6).

- **DFY** minimizes Effort & Sacrifice and Time Delay (highest value, highest price)
- **DWY** reduces Effort & Sacrifice partially while maintaining Perceived Likelihood through guidance
- **DIY** requires the most Effort & Sacrifice but scales infinitely at low cost

"Beginner marketers focus on bigger claims (top of equation). Advanced marketers focus on reducing time delay and effort (bottom of equation) -- like Apple, Amazon, Netflix" ($100M Offers Ch.6).

**The London Underground Example**: If a problem persists, all the logical solutions have likely been tried because they are obvious. What remains are psychological solutions. The London underground improved satisfaction more with a cheap dotted map showing wait times (reducing perceived wait) than with billions spent on faster trains (reducing actual wait). When choosing delivery format, consider whether the bottleneck is the actual delivery or the customer's perception of it. A perception-driven delivery change (making something feel easier or faster) can outperform an actual delivery improvement ($100M Offers Ch.6).

### The Product Delivery Cheat Codes (Delivery Cube)

From $100M Offers Ch.10. Six dimensions for brainstorming how to deliver each solution. Use ALL six when designing delivery vehicles -- do not default to the obvious format.

**Dimension 1 -- Level of Personal Attention**
- One-on-one
- Small group
- One-to-many

**Dimension 2 -- Level of Effort Required from Client**
- DIY: they figure it out themselves
- DWY: you teach and guide them
- DFY: you do it for them

**Dimension 3 -- Live Delivery Medium**
- In-person
- Phone / Zoom
- Email / text / chat

**Dimension 4 -- Recorded Consumption Format**
- Audio
- Video
- Written

**Dimension 5 -- Response Speed and Availability**
- 24/7
- Business hours (9-5)
- Within 5 minutes / within an hour / within 24 hours

**Dimension 6 -- The 10x to 1/10th Stretch Test**
- "If my customers paid me 10x my price (or $100,000), what would I provide?"
- "If they paid me 1/10th the price and I had to make my product even more valuable, how would I do that?"

"These extremes stretch your thinking in both directions and reveal creative solutions you would never consider at your current price point" ($100M Offers Ch.10).

At 10x: you would fly to their location, live with them, and do everything for them.
At 1/10th: you would create automated tools, pre-built templates, and self-service systems. The 1/10th version often reveals scalable solutions that are high value and low cost.

### The Sales-to-Fulfillment Continuum

From $100M Offers Ch.10. The guiding principle for sequencing delivery decisions:

**"Create flow, monetize flow, then add friction."**

1. Over-deliver like crazy at first to create cash flow
2. Use cash flow to fix operations and create efficiency
3. You may not even need to change what you offer -- just create systems that deliver the same value at lower cost to you

The more you do for clients (DFY), the easier it is to sell but harder to fulfill. The less you do (DIY), the harder it is to sell but easier to fulfill. Start by over-delivering to generate flow, then optimize toward scalability.

### One-to-Many Scalability Insight

From $100M Offers Ch.10: "High value one-to-many solutions (tools, templates, recorded content) have high one-time creation cost but infinitely low ongoing cost -- this is why software becomes so valuable."

This is the key insight for scaling delivery: invest heavily once in creating assets (templates, tools, recorded trainings, automated systems), then deliver them to unlimited customers at near-zero marginal cost.

### The Four Offer Types (Context for Delivery Decisions)

From $100M Money Models. There are exactly four offer types that compose a Money Model. Understanding their purpose helps inform delivery design, but the books do not prescribe a fixed delivery model (DIY/DWY/DFY) for each type -- the right delivery format depends on your market, price point, and stage.

- **Attraction Offers** turn strangers into customers using free or discounted entry points. Examples include giveaways, decoy offers, "win your money back" challenges, and "buy X get Y free" ($100M Money Models).
- **Upsell Offers** get existing customers to spend more by solving the next problem that appears after the first is solved. Four types: Classic, Menu (with Unselling, Prescription, A/B, and Card On File), Anchor (show 5-10x price first), and Rollover (credit past purchases) ($100M Money Models).
- **Downsell Offers** turn nos into yeses by changing how they pay or what they get -- never the same thing for cheaper. Includes Payment Plan Downsells (7-step process), Trial With Penalty, and Feature Downsells (remove features from highest to lowest value) ($100M Money Models).
- **Continuity Offers** keep people buying with ongoing value for ongoing payments. Make this the LAST offer in the sequence -- continuity on its own leaves you cash-strapped ($100M Money Models).

Build Money Models one stage at a time. Stage I: Get Cash with Attraction Offers. Stage II: Get More Cash with Upsell and Downsell Offers. Stage III: Get The Most Cash with Continuity Offers ($100M Money Models).

### Billing Cadence and Continuity Delivery

From $100M Money Models, based on data from 14,000 businesses: Monthly billing = 10.7% monthly churn, Quarterly = 5%, Annual = 2%. When designing delivery for Continuity Offers, the billing cadence directly affects retention and should be factored into the delivery model. Start high (fewer, bigger payments) and work your way down ($100M Money Models).

Continuity pricing ratios for standalone vs. continuity: 1.33x standalone price = 50% choose continuity, 2x = 70%, 2.66x = 90%. People pay 33% more to avoid continuity -- so charge 33% more for one-time and half will still buy the continuity option ($100M Money Models).

## Step-by-Step Process

### Step 1: Map the Outcome to Value Equation Drivers

Extract from the user:
- What result is promised (Dream Outcome)
- How complex the delivery is (affects Effort & Sacrifice)
- How fast it needs to happen (Time Delay)
- What proof exists that it works (Perceived Likelihood)

Then assess: which Value Equation driver is the current bottleneck? If customers are not buying because the result takes too much effort, the delivery model needs to shift toward DFY. If they doubt it will work, the delivery needs more personal attention (DWY with coaching).

Consider whether the bottleneck is actual or perceptual. The London underground example shows that a perception-driven change (making something feel easier) can outperform an actual delivery improvement.

### Step 2: Profile the Customer

Identify:
- Skill level (can they execute DIY?)
- Available time (do they have bandwidth for DWY?)
- Willingness to learn vs. willingness to pay
- Urgency of the problem (high urgency favors DFY)

### Step 3: Run the Delivery Cube

For each solution the offer promises, brainstorm delivery vehicles across all six dimensions. Do not stop at the obvious format. Generate at least 3-5 options per solution by varying the dimensions.

Example for "help them create a marketing plan":
- One-on-one, DFY, Zoom, video recording, 24hr response = Premium consulting
- Small group, DWY, Zoom, written templates, business hours = Cohort program
- One-to-many, DIY, email, written + video, 24hr response = Self-paced course with templates

### Step 4: Apply the 10x / 1-10th Stretch Test

For each solution, ask both questions:

**10x question**: "If they paid $100,000, what would I provide?" This reveals the ultimate DFY version -- maximum personal attention, fastest delivery, zero client effort.

**1/10th question**: "If they paid 1/10th and I had to make it MORE valuable, how?" This reveals scalable one-to-many solutions -- templates, tools, automations, recorded content that cost once and deliver forever.

Document both extremes. The real delivery vehicle usually sits between them, borrowing elements from each.

### Step 5: Trim and Stack

From $100M Offers Ch.10:
- Remove high-cost, low-value delivery items
- Keep low-cost, high-value delivery items
- Name every component and assign dollar values to each ($100M Offers Ch.10, Step 5 of Grand Slam Offer process)
- Stack everything into the final deliverable

Prioritize one-to-many solutions with high one-time creation cost but near-zero ongoing cost. These create the highest margins.

### Step 6: Choose the Primary Model

Based on Steps 1-5, select the primary delivery model:

**Choose DIY when:**
- The problem is simple enough for self-service
- The customer enjoys learning and has time
- Price must be low or the offer needs to reduce friction for new customers
- Scalability is the primary goal

**Choose DWY when:**
- Guidance meaningfully improves success rates
- Accountability matters for the outcome
- Medium price point works for the market
- The offer is a core revenue driver

**Choose DFY when:**
- Speed is critical to the customer
- The task is complex and requires expertise
- The customer lacks skill or time
- Willingness to pay is high
- The offer is a premium offering

### Step 7: Design the Hybrid and Offer Ladder

Combine formats across tiers to maximize the Value Equation at each price point:

- **Entry (DIY)**: One-to-many, templates + recorded content, async support
- **Core (DWY)**: Small group, live coaching + templates, business-hours response
- **Premium (DFY)**: One-on-one, full implementation, fast response time

Each tier should increase personal attention and decrease client effort while increasing price. This creates an upgrade path that matches the customer's evolving needs.

### Step 8: Engineer Fast Emotional Wins into Delivery

From $100M Leads and Gym Launch Secrets. Design your onboarding to create a big emotional win as early as possible -- ideally within the first 48 hours. Customers form their lasting impression of a business within the first 48 hours after purchase ($100M Leads).

"Always try and incorporate short-term, immediate wins for a client. They just need to know they are on the right path and that they made the right decision trusting you" (Gym Launch Secrets).

This affects delivery model choice directly:
- DFY can deliver first wins fastest (you do the work, they see results immediately)
- DWY needs structured onboarding with quick-win milestones built in
- DIY needs the strongest quick-win design -- the first module or template should produce a visible result within the first session

Practical guidance: deliver progress updates frequently. Break big deliverables into small milestones. Add 50% to timelines so you always deliver early. Never expect customers to forgive you for slow starts ($100M Leads).

### Step 9: Apply the Sales-to-Fulfillment Continuum

Sequence the rollout:
1. Launch with over-delivery (lean toward DFY elements even at lower tiers)
2. Generate cash flow and collect testimonials
3. Systematize delivery -- replace personal attention with one-to-many assets where possible
4. Optimize margins without reducing perceived value

"Create flow, monetize flow, then add friction."

## Key Principles

- **DFY = bottom of Value Equation approaches zero.** When the customer does nothing and the result appears, perceived value is maximized. This is why DFY commands the highest prices ($100M Offers Ch.6).

- **One-to-many is the scalability unlock.** "High value one-to-many solutions have high one-time creation cost but infinitely low ongoing cost." Invest in creating assets once -- templates, tools, recordings -- and deliver them infinitely ($100M Offers Ch.10).

- **"Create flow, monetize flow, then add friction."** Over-deliver first to generate demand and cash. Then systematize. Do not optimize delivery before you have customers ($100M Offers Ch.10).

- **The 10x/1-10th test prevents tunnel vision.** Imagining both extremes reveals delivery vehicles you would never consider at your current price point. The 1/10th version often produces the highest-margin scalable solution ($100M Offers Ch.10).

- **People pay for effortless solutions even when less effective.** "People pay $200 for supplements but will not pay $29/month for a gym membership -- because the perceived value (low effort, low time) is higher for supplements even though the gym is objectively better." Capitalize on how people actually behave, not how they should behave ($100M Offers Ch.6).

- **Perception-driven delivery changes can outperform actual improvements.** The London underground dotted map example: a cheap map reducing perceived wait beat billions spent on faster trains. When designing delivery, consider whether a psychological improvement (making something feel easier, faster, or more certain) is the real lever ($100M Offers Ch.6).

- **Force first wins in 48 hours.** Customers form their lasting impression within the first 48 hours after purchase. Set many expectations, meet many expectations, repeat. The delivery model must be designed to produce visible results in this window ($100M Leads).

- **Name every component and assign dollar values.** When stacking delivery into the final offer, give each component a name and a dollar value. The stacked presentation with assigned values makes the total value far exceed the price, making it impossible to compare to competitors ($100M Offers Ch.10).

## Common Mistakes

- **Optimizing delivery before having customers.** The Sales-to-Fulfillment Continuum says to create flow first. Over-deliver, generate cash, then optimize. Premature systematization kills businesses that never got traction.

- **Defaulting to DIY because it scales.** DIY has the lowest perceived value and lowest completion rates. If the customer needs the result fast and lacks skill, DIY will kill conversions even if it is cheaper to deliver.

- **Ignoring the 1/10th side of the stretch test.** Most people only think about the 10x premium version. The 1/10th version -- "how would I make this MORE valuable at a fraction of the price?" -- reveals the scalable one-to-many assets that create the highest margins.

- **Treating delivery models as fixed.** The same product can be delivered as DIY, DWY, or DFY depending on the tier. Design an offer ladder, not a single delivery format. "It is less about having a hundred different products with the same offer, and more about having a hundred different offers for the same product" ($100M Money Models).

- **Discounting instead of changing delivery.** When customers say the price is too high, do not lower the price -- change the delivery model. Lower prices by changing what customers get, not by discounting. Remove features from highest to lowest value. People see the value in what you removed after seeing the price difference. This is a Feature Downsell, not a discount ($100M Money Models).

- **Neglecting response speed as a value lever.** Dimension 5 of the Delivery Cube (response speed) is one of the easiest ways to differentiate tiers. 24/7 response for premium, business hours for core, 24-hour response for entry -- same content, different access.

- **No quick wins in onboarding.** If customers do not experience a visible result within the first 48 hours, they form a negative impression that is hard to reverse. Design the delivery model so the first thing the customer receives produces an immediate, visible outcome ($100M Leads).

- **Using logical solutions when psychological ones would work.** If the delivery problem persists despite trying obvious fixes, consider whether the solution is psychological -- changing how the customer perceives the delivery rather than changing the delivery itself. "All the logical solutions have already been tried. What remains are psychological solutions" ($100M Offers Ch.6).
